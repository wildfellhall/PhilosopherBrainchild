## Results: 
The human mind, in its incessant pursuit of all that is enigmatic pertaining to its own existence, often fails to arrive at the conclusion that, in order to explain what it is and whence it comes, it has merely to appeal to the only thing that it knows—namely, itself. But this is just what it shuns, because in so doing it would be obliged to admit that it is the creator of all the categories of thought with which it seeks to comprehend itself. As a matter of fact, it is quite impossible to explain this cognition, with all its peculiarities, by any means other than those furnished by the cognition  itself. We may, it is true, attempt to explain it by the analogy of phenomena, or of other forms of nature, or even by a reference to an intelligent cause, which is the substratum of our own ego; but we must never  forget that all these are mere analogies, and that we cannot, by their aid, discover a single one of the properties which constitute the essence of the soul. Thus we can never hope to reach the soul in its transcendental purity, or to attain to any clear insight into the mode in which it is affected by things, or how it is connected with the world of phenomena.

On the subject of romance, I have this laconic point to make, for it is not within my purview to critique the feelings with which ministrations are bestowed or received:  that the sentiment in which we listen to the lover’s tales is one of curiosity, not of sympathy. It is rather as if a man should come to you and say, ‘I have a pain in my arm which I can describe to you, which you have felt some day or other, and which you cannot help feeling as you listen to me.’ Such is the common experience of lovers, which the poets have made their own. They tell us that love is the acme of human felicity . It may be so; but we know it to be a transient state, and therefore not the highest which man can enjoy. For, if we take a survey of human life as a whole, it cannot be doubted that the highest happiness is derived from the exercise of the higher faculties, and from the contemplation of the divine nature. The state of love, if we may judge from common experience, is not a state of contemplation, but of feeling. The lover is not occupied in the contemplation of the perfections of his mistress; he is absorbed in the feeling of his love for her. The object of his thoughts is her person or her actions, not the divine nature; and the impression of these is rather upon his senses than upon his understanding. If we except the passion of religious fervour, it is the only state of mind which is wholly absorbed in the sense of being alive. It is the only state in which we feel our own existence.
